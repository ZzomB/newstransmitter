## 📖 Main Content

The Baltic Sea region is rapidly becoming the primary theater for Russia's "gray-zone" warfare against the West. In a series of coordinated warnings, leaders from Lithuania, Latvia, and Poland have signaled that Moscow is actively planning physical attacks on critical infrastructure along NATO’s eastern flank.

According to Lithuanian President Gitanas Nausėda, intelligence assessments indicate a heightened risk of targeted sabotage aimed at disrupting key transit systems and energy links—specifically facilities connecting the Baltics to the broader European electricity grid. While the intelligence does not specify the exact timing or locations of these potential strikes, Nausėda emphasized that they could involve "limited kinetic operations" using both conventional and unconventional methods.

The response from neighboring states has been equally urgent. Polish Prime Minister Donald Tusk confirmed that Warsaw is preparing for various escalation scenarios, noting that the evolving dynamics of the war in Ukraine make the coming months highly volatile. Meanwhile, Poland recently intercepted Russian surveillance aircraft mapping air defense systems over the Baltic Sea, and Polish Foreign Minister Radosław Sikorski issued a blunt warning directly to Moscow: *"We know what you are planning. Don’t do it."*

In Moscow, Kremlin spokesperson Dmitry Peskov flatly rejected the allegations. He accused Baltic leaders of fabricating threats to brainwash their domestic populations and pave the way for further NATO militarization in the region.

However, Baltic officials point to a long and well-documented history of Russian hybrid operations. Just last winter, a cyberattack attributed to Russia’s FSB Center 16 targeted a Polish combined heat and power plant, threatening utilities for half a million citizens. Additionally, maritime think tanks have reported the use of Russian "shadow ships" to deploy drones disrupting civilian aviation, while domestic drone scares have already triggered political fallout—including the resignation of Latvian Prime Minister Evika Siliņa over her government's handling of stray drone incursions.

## 💡 Key Takeaways & Analysis

### 1. The Strategy of "Below-the-Threshold" Aggression
By focusing on "limited kinetic operations" rather than overt military invasions, Moscow aims to operate in the gray zone of international conflict. A physical attack on a power substation or a rail line is devastating to local populations, yet it is difficult to cleanly attribute and may not immediately trigger a full-scale military retaliation under NATO’s Article 5. This strategic ambiguity allows Russia to inflict economic and psychological damage while keeping Western allies divided on how to respond.

### 2. Testing the Alliance’s Resolve
As Ukraine continues to strike deep into Russian territory, Moscow is looking for asymmetric ways to pressure the West. By targeting Poland and the Baltics, Russia is testing the operational readiness of European infrastructure security. If a Russian sabotage operation succeeds without a unified, decisive response from NATO and the European Union, it weakens the credibility of the West’s collective security guarantees.

### 3. The Vulnerability of Regional Grid Decoupling
The timing of these threats is not coincidental. The Baltic states have been aggressively working to desynchronize their electricity grids from the Soviet-era BRELL system and integrate fully with continental Europe. This transition period is highly sensitive. Any successful sabotage of these electricity and transit links could stall Baltic energy independence and leave the region economically vulnerable.

### 4. Psychological Warfare and Democratic Erosion
As Lithuania’s Chief of Defense, Gen. Raimundas Vaikšnoras, pointed out, the ultimate objective of these operations is often psychological. By creating a persistent state of anxiety—whether through GPS jamming, sudden drone alerts in capitals like Vilnius, or threats to heating systems—Russia aims to erode public trust in local governments and democratic institutions. Moving forward, Baltic and Polish defense strategies must focus not just on military hardware, but on reinforcing civil resilience and public readiness for hybrid contingencies.
