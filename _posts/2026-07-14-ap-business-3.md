## 📌 Key Summary
* **The Incident:** Tokyo, a five-year-old black Labrador, suffered acute cannabis poisoning and collapsed near the summit of Ben Nevis, Britain’s highest mountain.
* **The Rescue:** A volunteer mountain rescue team carried the incapacitated 55-pound dog down a steep, rain-slicked trail on a stretcher to receive emergency veterinary care.
* **The Diagnosis:** Vets treated Tokyo with activated charcoal to combat the neurotoxin, leading to a full recovery overnight but landing her owner with a £1,000 ($1,335) medical bill.
* **The Broader Trend:** This alarming episode highlights a sharp, legalization-fueled spike in accidental pet cannabis poisonings across both the United Kingdom and the United States.

## 📖 Main Content

For Christina Bluhme, an experienced dog trainer of 25 years, a July hike up Scotland’s Ben Nevis was supposed to be a memorable adventure with her son and her dogs. Instead, it devolved into a terrifying medical emergency at 4,413 feet.

Near the summit of the UK's tallest peak, Bluhme's five-year-old black Labrador, Tokyo, began displaying alarming neurological symptoms. The dog started swaying unsteadily as if intoxicated, quickly lost the ability to stand, and eventually collapsed. To make matters worse, the pleasant weather evaporated, replaced by freezing rain and temperatures plunging to 5°C (41°F).

Realizing they had to abort their ascent immediately, Bluhme and her son Magnus attempted to carry the 55-pound (25 kg) dog down. Fortuitously, their calls for help reached the Lochaber Mountain Rescue Team. The all-volunteer crew, already descending from a separate helicopter evacuation, sprang into action. Placing Tokyo on a rescue stretcher, the team ran down the treacherous, wet, and rocky trail, reaching the trailhead in just about an hour.

At Crown Vets in Fort William, veterinary staff initially suspected a spinal injury due to Tokyo’s loss of motor control and drifting consciousness. However, consultation with a poison control center confirmed a different culprit: cannabis toxicity. Tokyo had likely eaten discarded cannabis or cannabis-infused products left by hikers on the trail.

After receiving activated charcoal to bind the toxins in her digestive tract, Tokyo made a swift, complete recovery by the next morning. While Bluhme was relieved to have her healthy dog back, the ordeal cost her a hefty £1,000 ($1,335) in emergency veterinary fees—and served as a stark warning to pet owners everywhere.

## 💡 Key Takeaways & Analysis

### 1. The Unintended Consequences of Cannabis Legalization and Decriminalization
While the legalization and social acceptance of cannabis have expanded globally, public spaces have become minefields for domestic animals. Dogs are natural foragers with incredibly sensitive olfactory systems. Whether it is discarded joint rots, vaporized cartridges, or highly concentrated edible gummies left in parks and on hiking trails, dogs will easily find and consume them. Because THC (the psychoactive compound in cannabis) is highly toxic to dogs, even microscopic amounts can trigger a severe reaction.

### 2. A Skyrocketing Veterinary Crisis
This is not an isolated incident. In the United States, where recreational marijuana is legal in dozens of states, pet poisoning statistics have spiked dramatically:
* In 2023, marijuana and other recreational drugs entered the **ASPCA’s Top 10 list of pet toxins** for the first time.
* The ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center reported a **10% increase** in cannabis-related calls in 2023 compared to the previous year.
* Over a five-year span, these poison control calls have surged **nearly threefold**.

### 3. The Difficulty of Diagnosis
Even experienced pet professionals struggle to recognize the signs of THC poisoning. Symptoms in dogs often mimic severe neurological trauma:
* Loss of bodily coordination (ataxia) and stumbling
* Extreme lethargy or, conversely, hyper-excitability
* Slow heart rate, dilated pupils, and urinary incontinence
* Drifting in and out of consciousness

Because pet owners are often unaware that their dog has consumed something on a trail, veterinary teams frequently lose precious time testing for spinal injuries or stroke before diagnosing poisoning.

### 4. Practical Protection for Pet Owners
As outdoor spaces become more heavily trafficked by recreational users, pet owners must adapt:
* **Master the "Leave It" Command:** Solidifying this command can save a dog from consuming toxic items on the ground.
* **Carry First-Aid Essentials:** In remote areas, having the contact information of veterinary poison hotlines and knowing the location of the nearest emergency vet clinic is critical.
* **Public Education:** Hikers and campers must be educated that "leave no trace" applies to cannabis products as well. Biodegradable or not, discarding edibles or joints in nature poses a direct, life-threatening hazard to local wildlife and companion animals.
