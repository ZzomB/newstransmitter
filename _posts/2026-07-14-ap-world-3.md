## 📌 Key Summary
* **The Incident:** Tokyo, a five-year-old black Labrador, collapsed near the summit of Ben Nevis—the UK’s highest peak—after accidentally ingesting discarded cannabis on the trail.
* **The Rescue:** Under freezing and rainy conditions, an all-volunteer mountain rescue team transported the 55-pound dog down the steep terrain on a stretcher.
* **The Treatment:** Veterinarians diagnosed Tokyo with cannabis toxicity and administered activated charcoal; she made a complete recovery overnight.
* **The Broader Trend:** This remote incident reflects a sharp, global rise in pet cannabis poisoning, driven by legalization and changing social habits.

## 📖 Main Content

On July 5, Christina Bluhme—a professional dog trainer with 25 years of experience—embarked on a hike up Scotland’s highest mountain, Ben Nevis, with her son Magnus and her five-year-old black Labrador, Tokyo. What was meant to be a scenic trek quickly turned into a high-altitude emergency when they neared the 4,413-foot (1,345-meter) summit.

As the weather deteriorated into rain and temperatures plummeted to a frigid 5°C (41°F), Tokyo’s behavior abruptly changed. The 55-pound dog began swaying like she was intoxicated, lost her coordination, and ultimately collapsed, unable to stand or walk.

Recognizing the gravity of the situation, Bluhme abandoned their push to the summit. Fortunately, the Lochaber Mountain Rescue Team, an all-volunteer unit, happened to be descending the mountain after assisting with a separate helicopter evacuation. The rescuers secured Tokyo onto a stretcher and rapidly carried her down the slick, rocky trail, reaching the trailhead in just about an hour.

Upon arrival at Crown Vets in Fort William, the veterinary team initially suspected a spinal injury. However, as Tokyo drifted in and out of consciousness, veterinarians recognized the signs of neurotoxicity. Consulting with a poison control center confirmed that Tokyo was suffering from cannabis intoxication, likely caused by eating discarded marijuana leftovers or edibles along the hiking trail.

After receiving activated charcoal, a standard treatment to bind toxins in the stomach, Tokyo made a swift and full recovery by the following morning. While Bluhme was left with a £1,000 ($1,335) veterinary bill, she expressed profound relief and immense gratitude for the volunteers who saved her dog's life.

## 💡 Key Takeaways & Analysis

While Tokyo's story ended happily, it shines a spotlight on a rapidly growing and overlooked public health issue: the skyrocketing rate of accidental cannabis poisoning in domestic pets.

### 1. The Cost of Legalization and Changing Social Habits
As cannabis legalization spreads across the United States and decriminalization or tolerance grows in the United Kingdom, marijuana has transitioned from a hidden substance to a mainstream consumer product. This increased accessibility has had a direct spillover effect on pets.

According to the American Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals (ASPCA), marijuana entered the top 10 list of pet toxins for the first time in 2023. Calls to the ASPCA’s poison control center regarding cannabis ingestion rose by 10% in a single year and have surged nearly threefold over the past five years. As people increasingly consume cannabis in public spaces—including national parks and hiking trails—improperly discarded joints, roaches, and THC-infused edibles are becoming dangerous hazards for curious dogs.

### 2. Why Cannabis is Highly Toxic to Dogs
Many pet owners mistakenly assume that because cannabis is relatively safe for human recreational use, it cannot harm animals. In reality, dogs are incredibly sensitive to Tetrahydrocannabinol (THC), the psychoactive compound in marijuana.
* **Biological Sensitivity:** Dogs have a much higher concentration of cannabinoid receptors in their brains than humans do.
* **Severe Symptoms:** Even small amounts of THC can cause ataxia (loss of coordination), severe lethargy, urinary incontinence, dilated pupils, slowed heart rate, and in extreme cases, seizures or comas.
* **The "Edible" Compound Danger:** Edibles pose a double threat. If a dog ingests a cannabis brownie or chocolate bar, they are hit with a dual dose of THC and theobromine (the highly toxic compound in chocolate), which can easily prove fatal.

### 3. The Value of Community Support Systems
This incident also highlights the indispensable role of volunteer emergency services. The Lochaber Mountain Rescue Team, though primarily trained for human search and rescue, did not hesitate to treat Tokyo's emergency with the same urgency as a human medical crisis. As outdoor recreation rises, these volunteer-run organizations face increased strain, reminding us of the need to support and fund local rescue operations.

### Summary for Pet Owners
Even seasoned professionals like Bluhme can be caught off guard by pet poisoning. Hikers and pet owners must remain vigilant, keeping a close eye on what their dogs sniff out in public parks and trails. If you suspect your pet has ingested cannabis, seeking immediate veterinary care is vital. When treated promptly, the prognosis for recovery is excellent, but leaving it untreated in harsh outdoor environments can quickly turn a "bad trip" into a tragedy.
